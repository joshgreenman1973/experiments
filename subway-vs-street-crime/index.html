Is the Subway Safer Than the Street?

A per-hour risk model comparing violent crime victimization underground vs. on the surface in New York City.

Building on “Underground, Safer Than the Surface” (Vital City) with additional data from the American Time Use Survey, NYC DOT Citywide Mobility Survey, MIT/Nature Cities pedestrian model (2026), and MTA ridership data.

On raw counts, the subway accounts for less than 1 in 15 public-space violent crimes. But raw counts are meaningless without a denominator. What matters is: how much time do people spend in each environment?

How Vital City Framed It

Before extending the analysis, it’s worth walking through the original methodology carefully. The Vital City article took a valuable first step that most crime coverage skips entirely: it asked about exposure.

2Estimate exposure
3Compute rates
4Compare

Step 1: Isolating Public-Space Violence

The article focused on violent index crimes—murders, rapes, robberies, and felony assaults—comparing only those that occurred in the transit system to those on streets and public outdoor spaces. This is the right comparison: it excludes domestic violence, commercial burglaries, and other crimes that occur in contexts where subway-vs.-street framing makes no sense.

Step 2: Estimating Subway Exposure

The subway side is relatively tractable. In 2023, riders took roughly 1.3 billion trips. Each trip involves an average of ~30 minutes on a train plus ~10 minutes of platform time, stair-climbing, and tunnel-walking. That yields about 880 million person-hours of subway exposure per year.

A note on “person-hours.” This denominator captures total time everyone collectively spends in the subway system. It doesn’t matter whether those 880 million hours come from 5 million daily riders taking two trips each or from 2 million riders taking five trips each. What matters for risk calculation is: for every hour anyone is down there, how many crimes occur? A frequent rider accumulates more hours and proportionally more exposure—the rate per hour stays the same.

Step 3: The Key Insight

The article calculated a subway violent crime rate of roughly 1.3 crimes per million person-hours. Then it asked: what would the street rate need to be to equal this? The answer: New Yorkers would need to collectively spend 12.5+ billion person-hours on the streets each year for the per-hour risk to match the subway’s. That would require every person in the metro area to spend 25+ hours a week outdoors on the street—an implausible amount of time.

Step 4: Where I Extend the Analysis

The Vital City article made a compelling qualitative case, but stopped short of computing an actual street crime rate. My analysis below takes the next step: building three independent estimates of how many person-hours New Yorkers actually spend on streets, so we can calculate a direct per-hour comparison and let you stress-test the assumptions.

Estimating Street Exposure: The Hard Part

The subway estimate is relatively tight. The street side is where this analysis gets interesting—and where most existing analyses fall short.

Three Independent Estimates

Method 1: Walking-Trip Decomposition
NYC DOT’s Citywide Mobility Survey finds New Yorkers average 2.5 trips/weekday (2.3 on weekends). MIT’s Nature Cities model (2026) finds 41% of all NYC trips are on foot. With an effective daily population of ~9.5M (8.3M residents + 1M+ commuters + 200K tourists), that yields ~9.7M walking trips/day at ~20 min each = ~3.2M person-hours/day, or ~1.18B/year from walking trips alone.
Method 2: Time-Use Approach
ATUS shows Americans average ~20 min/day walking. NYCers walk 1.5–2.5× the national average (DOH, step-count studies). Using 45 min/day for the 8.3M residents, 30 min for 1M commuters, and 120 min for tourists yields ~2.5B person-hours/year. But this includes all outdoor time, not just street-adjacent exposure—discount by ~30% for time in parks, plazas, and private outdoor spaces to get ~1.7B for street-comparable spaces.
Method 3: Residual-Time / Activity Accounting
Of 24 hours, the average NYCer spends ~8 hrs sleeping, ~8–9 hrs at work/school (indoors), ~4–5 hrs at home awake, ~0.7 hrs on transit. That leaves ~2–3 hrs for “other”—errands, walking, socializing, dining out, etc. Not all is street-exposed; estimate ~60% is, yielding ~1.2–1.8 hrs/day × 9.5M = ~4.2–6.2B person-hours/year. But much of this is in cars, buildings, or otherwise sheltered. Applying a 30–40% “true street exposure” factor: ~1.5–2.2B.

I use 1.6 billion person-hours as the central estimate, with a range of 1.2B (conservative) to 2.2B (generous). This is roughly 1.8× the subway’s 880M—meaning the average New Yorker spends less than twice as much time in street-exposed spaces as on the subway system.

Neighborhood adjustment applies to street risk only. The subway is a system-wide network—a Mott Haven resident rides through Midtown on the same train as an Upper East Sider—so transit crime doesn’t vary cleanly by neighborhood. But surface crime rates vary enormously: a resident of Mott Haven faces 3× the citywide average violent crime risk per hour outdoors, while someone in Bayside faces about a quarter of it. That’s a 12× gap within the same city.

Why the Gap? Six Hypotheses

A risk ratio this large demands explanation. Several mechanisms likely contribute, and they reinforce each other.

1. Surveillance density. The subway is one of the most camera-saturated environments in New York. Platform cameras, in-car cameras, and uniformed officers at turnstiles create a deterrence environment that open streets cannot match. Offenders know this.
2. Channelized movement. Subway riders flow through controlled chokepoints—turnstiles, platform edges, car doors. Offenders have fewer escape routes and victims have more bystanders nearby. Streets offer infinite approach and escape vectors.
3. Population composition effects. Subway riders skew toward commuters—employed, sober, purposeful travelers. Street populations include all of these plus people in crisis, intoxicated individuals, and those involved in street-level disputes. The “interaction pool” on streets includes more potential offenders and more high-risk encounters.
4. Temporal concentration. Subway ridership concentrates heavily in rush hours, when violence is lowest. Streets have 24/7 exposure including late-night and early-morning hours when violence peaks. The subway’s temporal profile naturally avoids the highest-risk hours.
5. Dispute geography. Most violent crime arises from disputes, robberies, and drug markets—activities overwhelmingly situated on the surface. The subway is a transit conduit, not a place where people live, deal, argue, or drink. It’s inherently less “criminogenic” as an environment.
6. Geographic uniformity as a safety feature. This may be the most underappreciated factor. Above ground, crime risk is hyper-localized: a block in Brownsville can be 20× more dangerous than a block in Park Slope. Savvy New Yorkers navigate this geography instinctively—avoiding certain blocks, crossing the street, staying in well-lit areas. But the subway flattens geography. When you swipe in at a station, you enter a single connected system. A rider on the A train passes through Midtown and East New York in the same trip. You can’t “avoid the bad neighborhoods” underground the way you can on foot—but you don’t need to, because the system’s overall risk is so much lower. Half of all violent subway crimes concentrate in just 30 of 472 stations (6.4%), but even those high-crime stations have per-hour rates far below their surrounding streets. The subway’s geographic uniformity means its average rate is close to its typical rate—unlike the street, where the average conceals enormous local variation and the worst blocks drive much of the total.

What This Model Cannot Tell You

Reporting bias. Subway crime may be reported at higher rates (more cameras, more witnesses, more police presence) than street crime, which would make the true gap even larger than measured.

Crime type matters. The subway has a different crime mix—proportionally more robberies, fewer assaults from interpersonal disputes. A model broken down by crime type would yield different ratios for different offenses.

Geography and time. Both rates vary enormously by station/neighborhood and time of day. A per-hour comparison at 2 AM in East New York vs. Grand Central at rush hour would look very different from these system-wide averages.

Perception vs. reality. The enclosed, inescapable nature of subway cars may make people feel less safe even when they are statistically safer. Fear of crime and actual crime risk are related but distinct phenomena.

Exposure measurement. The street person-hour estimate is the weakest link in this analysis. Better data—perhaps from the MIT/Nature Cities pedestrian model—could narrow the uncertainty range considerably.

Bottom Line

6–11×
Per hour spent, the street is six to eleven times more dangerous than the subway across all plausible assumptions

The popular fear of subway crime is understandable—an enclosed underground train feels dangerous. But the data tell a consistent story: hour for hour, New Yorkers face dramatically higher violent crime risk on the surface than underground. Even stress-testing every assumption in the most generous direction, the subway’s per-hour risk never comes close to the street’s.

This isn’t an argument that subway crime doesn’t matter. Every violent crime matters. But if the goal is reducing New Yorkers’ overall exposure to violence, the surface—where 93.5% of public-space violence occurs—is where the largest gains are to be found.
