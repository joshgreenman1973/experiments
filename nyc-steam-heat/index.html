The city that runs on steam

New York City has one of the most steam-dependent building stocks in the world. About 80% of the city's residential buildings -- hundreds of thousands of apartments -- push heat through cast-iron radiators fed by steam from basement boilers. Here's how it works and why it persists.

How steam heat works in a building

A boiler in the basement heats water into steam. Pressure pushes the steam up through risers to radiators on every floor. As the steam releases heat into the room, it condenses back into water and returns to the boiler to be reheated.

Typical two-pipe steam system in a pre-war NYC apartment building

Why New York runs on steam

It's a product of the building boom. Most of NYC's apartment buildings were constructed between 1900 and 1940, when steam was the dominant heating technology. Builders ran cast-iron pipes and installed radiators by the thousands. Those systems are still in place today.

Steam is hard to replace. Ripping out steam risers and radiators from a six-story walk-up means opening walls on every floor, relocating pipes, and often displacing tenants. For a typical apartment building, a full conversion to hot water or electric heat can cost hundreds of thousands of dollars -- with no easy way to pass that cost along in a rent-regulated building.

So the systems persist. About 37% of the city's multifamily buildings use one-pipe steam, where a single pipe carries steam up and condensate back down. Another 25% use two-pipe steam. These systems are notoriously hard to balance -- tenants near the boiler bake while those on upper floors freeze, leading to the quintessential NYC scene: windows thrown open in January.

One-pipe vs. two-pipe: In a one-pipe system, steam and condensate share the same pipe -- the steam rises and the water trickles back down. It's simpler but harder to control. In a two-pipe system (shown in the diagram above), separate pipes carry steam up and water back, allowing better temperature regulation.

Con Ed's underground steam network

Beneath Manhattan's streets, 105 miles of pipe carry steam from six generating stations to about 1,300 buildings. It's the largest commercial district steam system in the world -- bigger than the next nine combined. The network runs from Battery Park to 96th Street on the west side and 89th Street on the east.

District steam customers include the Empire State Building, Rockefeller Center, Grand Central Terminal, the United Nations, and dozens of hospitals and museums. Unlike buildings with their own boilers, these buildings receive steam directly from Con Ed's plants.

A brief history

1882
The New York Steam Company begins service in lower Manhattan -- the same year Edison lit up Pearl Street with electric lights.
1900-1940
NYC's great building boom installs steam radiators in hundreds of thousands of apartments. The technology becomes the city's default.
1954
The New York Steam Company merges with Consolidated Edison.
2019
NYC passes Local Law 97, capping building emissions. Steam's relatively low carbon intensity gives it a near-term advantage over oil.
2024
Con Edison announces plans for electric boilers, heat pumps, and geothermal exploration to decarbonize the district steam system by 2040.
