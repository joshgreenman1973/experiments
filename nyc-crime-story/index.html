Chapter 1

Four eras, one city

Thirty-three years of NYPD data don’t tell a single story. They tell four — each with its own dynamics, its own winners and losers, and its own unresolved questions.

1993–2008
2009–2019
2020–21
2022–25

Era 1: The Great Decline (1993–2008). Major felonies fell from 430,460 to 116,952 — a 73% drop in 15 years. Every category plummeted. The city became, statistically, a different place.

Era 2: The Long Plateau (2009–2019). The headline numbers plateaued near historic lows. But beneath the surface, fractures were forming. Felony assault quietly reversed course. Grand larceny stopped falling.

Era 3: The Pandemic Rupture (2020–2021). COVID-19 shattered the plateau. Shootings doubled. Murder surged. Car theft exploded. But not all crimes spiked equally.

Era 4: The Uneven Recovery (2022–2025). Shootings and murder snapped back — then kept falling to historic lows. But assault climbed higher. Larceny remains stubbornly elevated. Two cities emerged: one safer than ever, one where certain crimes are worse than they’ve been in a generation.

Chapter 2 · 1993–2008

The great decline

In 1993, New York City recorded 430,460 major felony complaints. Fifteen years later, the number had fallen to 116,952. Every single category collapsed.

Murder fell 73%. Shootings fell 71%. Auto theft — the quintessential 1990s New York crime — plummeted an astonishing 89%, from 111,622 to 12,493. Burglary dropped 80%. Robbery dropped 74%.

This wasn’t just incremental improvement. Year after year, for 15 straight years, the numbers went down. The decline cut across boroughs, precincts, and crime types.

Chapter 3 · 2009–2025

The assault comeback

Here’s the story the headline numbers obscure: while murder and shootings kept falling, felony assault quietly reversed course — and has been rising for 17 years.

Felony assault bottomed out at 16,002 in 2008. Then it started climbing. By 2019, it had reached 20,695 — a 29% increase that went largely unnoticed beneath the broader crime-decline narrative. After the pandemic, the rise accelerated. By 2025, felony assault stands at 29,841 — 86% above its 2008 low and closing in on its 1993 level of 41,121.

Chapter 4

Murder and shootings: historic lows

If the assault story is sobering, the gun violence story is the opposite. Shootings in 2025 stand at 688 — the lowest figure in the entire 33-year dataset. That’s an 87% decline from 1993, and 11% below the pre-pandemic low of 776 in 2019.

The pattern is telling. Crimes of opportunity on empty streets — burglary, auto theft — surged. Gun violence exploded as courts closed and social services contracted. But grand larceny (which requires victims present to steal from) and harassment both fell because fewer people were out.

Chapter 6

The stubborn middle

Between the dramatic success of shootings and the troubling rise of assault, a cluster of crimes occupy an uncomfortable middle ground — substantially below their 1990s peaks, but elevated and refusing to fall further.

Car theft’s V-shaped recovery

Auto theft dropped 95% from 1993 to 2018 — from 111,622 to 5,428. It was the greatest single-category decline in the dataset. Then it reversed. By 2023, it had nearly tripled to 15,795. The Kia/Hyundai vulnerability, social media–driven theft rings, and reduced street-level deterrence all played roles. Even now, at 13,532, it sits 149% above its floor.

Grand larceny’s quiet persistence

Grand larceny — which includes phone snatching, package theft, and retail crime — dropped from 85,737 in 1993 to 37,838 in 2010. Then it started rising again, and it has stayed elevated: 48,107 in 2025. It’s now the single largest category of major felony, accounting for nearly 40% of the total.

Together, felony assault, grand larceny, and auto theft now represent the bulk of the city’s crime problem. The old 1990s crime mix — dominated by burglary and robbery — has been replaced by a new one.

Chapter 9

The arrest equation

While crime has fallen, the arrest rate for major felonies has risen sharply. In 1993, police made arrests in roughly 16% of major felony cases. Today, that figure is 46%.

15.9%
Arrest rate, 1993
46.0%
Arrest rate, 2025

Complaints plummeted from 430,460 to 121,756, while arrests fell more modestly from 68,357 to 56,039. Police are making fewer total arrests but clearing a much larger share of reported crimes. Notably, total arrests in 2024 and 2025 — over 116,000 across all categories — are at their highest levels in the dataset.

The picture today

Two cities in one

The data doesn’t paint a simple picture of progress or decline. It reveals a city that has split into two overlapping realities.

−87%
Shootings since 1993
+86%
Fel. assault since 2008

In one New York, shootings are at a 33-year low, murder is near a historic floor, and the pandemic spike is receding into memory. Burglary and robbery — the crimes that once defined daily life — have been reduced to fractions of their former selves.

In the other New York, felony assault is approaching levels not seen since the early 1990s. Grand larceny is stubbornly elevated. Car theft remains well above its pre-pandemic floor. And the geographic concentration of gun violence means the safety dividend has not been shared equally — seven of the ten most dangerous precincts for shootings are in a single borough.

The question is no longer whether New York got safer. It did — dramatically, measurably, historically. The question is why certain forms of violence are proving resistant to 33 years of progress, and what it would take to close the gap.
