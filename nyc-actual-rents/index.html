What New Yorkers Actually Pay in Rent

Real estate listings show a median asking rent near $3,500. But more than half the city’s rental housing is regulated. The actual median? Far lower—and the gap tells us something important about the market’s real structure.

Median

The exact midpoint of all rents paid. Half of households pay less, half pay more. Not distorted by extremes—the most honest single number for what “typical” means in housing.

Mean (Average)

Adds every dollar paid across all units and divides by the count. A handful of $15,000-a-month apartments in Manhattan can drag this number far above what most people experience.

The Full Picture
The Rent Spectrum: From Public Housing to Asking Price

New York’s rental market isn’t one market—it’s several, layered on top of each other. The bars below show the median rent for each major housing type, from NYCHA public housing to what landlords ask for unregulated vacancies.

57% of New York City’s rental units are regulated—rent-stabilized, rent-controlled, or public housing. Asking rents on listing sites reflect only the roughly 43% of units that turn over on the open market. The gap between these worlds is the defining fact of the city’s housing economy.

Methodology & Sources
Where This Data Comes From

Every number on this page is drawn from official government surveys and administrative records—not from listing sites, which capture only vacant, unregulated units seeking tenants. The key sources:

  • NYC Housing and Vacancy Survey (NYCHVS), 2023
    Mandated by state and city law, conducted by the U.S. Census Bureau. Covers a representative sample of all 2.2 million occupied rental units in New York City, including rent-stabilized, rent-controlled, and public housing. Selected Initial Findings (PDF)
  • NYC Rent Guidelines Board
    Publishes annual reports on rent-stabilized housing, including the Income and Affordability Study and the Income and Expense Study. The RGB sets annual rent adjustment percentages for stabilized leases. Research portal
  • NYCHA Fact Sheet, 2024
    Administrative data on New York City Housing Authority residents: average family rent ($621/month), average household income ($26,129/year), and unit counts across 335 developments. NYCHA Fact Sheet (PDF)
  • American Community Survey (ACS), U.S. Census Bureau
    Annual survey providing rent and income data at the census tract level. Distinguishes between contract rent and gross rent (which includes utilities). ACS 2023, Table B25064
  • NYC Comptroller’s Office
    Published a 2023 spotlight report explaining the structural gap between asking rents and actual rents paid, citing the role of rent regulation in anchoring the market. Spotlight: NYC’s Rental Housing Market
