The 30-Year Arc

U.S. firearm homicide rate per 100,000 population, 1993–2025

From crack-era peak to historic low — with a pandemic detour

Sources: Bureau of Justice Statistics, CDC NCHS, Council on Criminal Justice. 2025 figure is projected.

What happened: The firearm homicide rate peaked at ~7.0 per 100,000 in 1993 during the crack epidemic, then plunged over two decades to a low of ~3.5 in 2014. The pandemic-era spike in 2020–2021 drove rates back up to ~6.7 — nearly erasing 25 years of progress. But since 2022, homicides have fallen at record speed. If 2025 projections hold, the rate (~4.0) would be the lowest in over a century of record-keeping.

The pattern: American violence moves in waves. Each surge (Prohibition, the late '60s–'80s, crack, COVID) was followed by a decline — but this one is happening faster than any before. The 2025 projected rate of ~4.0 would match the post-WWII low of 1957, the calmest era in modern American history.

The Global Outlier

Even at historic lows, the U.S. gun homicide rate dwarfs peer nations

Gun homicide rate per 100,000 population

Sources: Everytown Research, World Population Review, Our World in Data. Most recent available year (2022–2024).

Scale matters: The U.S. rate of ~4.1–4.4 per 100,000 is 26× the average of other high-income nations. The key correlate: the U.S. has 120.5 guns per 100 residents — compared to 34.7 in Canada, 4.6 in the UK, and 0.3 in Japan. In 2024, 76% of all U.S. homicides involved a firearm, far above European or Asian averages. The U.S. rate is far below Brazil (~22) and Mexico (~17), but within the developed world, it remains a dramatic outlier.

Anatomy of Gun Deaths

Most gun deaths in America aren't what you think

57–58% — Suicide (27,593)
35% — Homicide (15,364)
4% — Law enforcement / Legal intervention
3% — Accidental
1% — Undetermined

Source: Giffords / CDC 2024 data. Total: ~44,447 gun deaths in 2024.

The suicide paradox: In 2024, firearm homicides fell 14.3% (from 17,927 to 15,364) while gun suicides actually increased 1.1% (from 27,300 to 27,593). Suicides now account for 57–58% of all gun deaths, up from 55% in 2023. The headline "gun deaths are falling" masks two diverging trends: a dramatic homicide decline alongside a rising suicide toll that current policies are failing to address.

Two Epidemics, Diverging

Firearm homicides vs. firearm suicides, 2019–2024

Homicides plummet while suicides quietly rise

Sources: Giffords, The Trace / CDC

The hidden crisis: Gun homicides dropped 14.3% in a single year (2023→2024) while gun suicides rose 1.1%. White males account for nearly 9 in 10 gun suicides, often in rural areas where community violence intervention programs don't reach. An alarming emerging trend: gun suicide rates among Black and Hispanic/Latino youth (ages 10–19) have tripled since 2014.

Who Bears the Burden

Gun violence is not equally distributed

Firearm homicide victimization rate by demographic (per 100,000), 2018–2022 avg

Source: Bureau of Justice Statistics, 2025

Stark disparities: In cities over 65,000, Black Americans face a gun homicide rate of 23.6 per 100,000 — a 5.5× disparity with white Americans (4.3). Young adults (18–24) face the highest risk by age. Males are victimized at 5.6× the rate of females. Despite this, Black Americans saw a 15% drop in their gun homicide rate in 2024, one of the sharpest declines. Handguns account for 90% of all firearm violence.

The Bottom Line

What the data actually tells us — and what it doesn't

The decline is real and historic

Three consecutive years of record-breaking drops. Multiple cities at 60-year lows. This isn't noise — it's a genuine, dramatic reversal of the pandemic-era spike.

It's broad-based

31 of 35 major cities saw declines in 2025. Robberies down 23%, carjackings down 43%. This isn't cherry-picked — nearly every category of violent crime is falling.

Context matters: compared to what?

Current rates are below 2019 pre-pandemic levels and approaching the 2014 modern low. But they remain far above other wealthy democracies and the 1950s-60s baseline. "Historic low" depends on your timeframe.

The U.S. remains an extreme outlier

Even at projected 2025 rates, America's gun homicide rate is 26× the average of other high-income nations. The decline narrows the gap with our own past, not with our peers.

Suicides aren't declining

Gun suicides — the majority of gun deaths — have actually risen, even as homicides plummet. The total gun death picture is less rosy than the homicide numbers alone suggest.

The funding cliff looms

The ARPA pandemic relief that funded CVI programs is expiring. Chicago invested $300M+ in community violence intervention since 2022, serving 27,000 high-risk individuals. Portland saw $5.26 return on every $1 invested. If this funding dries up in 2026, experts warn the decline could stall or reverse.
