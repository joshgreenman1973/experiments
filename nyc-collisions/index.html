02 — Key Findings
What the Data Reveals
Twelve years of collision data tell a story of paradoxes—fewer crashes, deadlier outcomes, and a city reshaped by new vehicles and a pandemic.
The COVID Paradox: Fewer Crashes, More Death
Loading…
Crashes fell 51% between 2018 and 2020. But fatalities rose from 231 to 269. The fatality rate per 1,000 crashes more than doubled—and has stayed elevated ever since. Empty streets meant higher speeds.
Fatality Rate Per 1,000 Crashes
Loading…
Pre-pandemic: roughly 1.1 fatal crashes per 1,000. By 2021 it hit 2.6 — the worst in the dataset. Even by 2024, it's 2.8. Each crash is now nearly 3× as likely to kill someone.
Vision Zero's Scorecard: Pedestrian Deaths
Loading…
Vision Zero launched in 2014. Pedestrian fatalities dropped from 176 in 2013 to 101 in 2020 — a 43% cut. But they've rebounded to the 120s since, erasing about half of the program's gains.
The E-Bike Explosion
Loading…
E-bike and e-scooter crashes went from near-zero to 4,065 in 2023 — a 3,800% increase in four years. Tracks directly with delivery app growth and micromobility adoption.
Cyclist Injuries: Structurally Higher
Loading…
Cyclist injuries rose 30% — from ~4,000/year (2013–2015) to ~5,200/year (2023–2025) — even as total crashes fell by more than half. More people cycling means more exposure, and infrastructure hasn't kept pace.
"Unsafe Speed" Rising in the Rankings
Loading…
"Unsafe Speed" entered the top 5 contributing factors post-pandemic, up from outside the top 5 before COVID. Consistent with the lethality spike — fewer cars on the road invites faster driving.
The Bronx: Growing Share of Fatalities
Loading…
In 2013–2015, the Bronx had 77 fatalities vs. Brooklyn's 201 (a 2.6:1 ratio). By 2022–2024, it's 92 vs. 150 (1.6:1). The Bronx's share of traffic deaths has grown even as its crash count fell, suggesting deeper infrastructure and enforcement gaps.
