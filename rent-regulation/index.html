Three claims, tested against the evidence
The advocate says

“Rent control protects tenants from displacement.”

The evidence says: Yes, for current tenants — at a cost. Diamond et al. (2019) found San Francisco rent control reduced tenant displacement by 20%. But landlords removed 15% of controlled units from the rental market, raising citywide rents 5.1%. The tenants who stay are protected; the tenants who need to find housing pay more.

The advocate says

“Modern rent regulations avoid the problems of old-style controls.”

The evidence says: They’re better, but not harmless. Germany’s Mietpreisbremse and Catalonia’s 2020 cap are “second-generation” controls. Both reduced controlled rents 4–5%. But both also triggered spillover rent increases in uncontrolled segments, and Catalonia saw new rental contracts fall 10%.

The critic says

“Rent control always destroys housing supply.”

The evidence says: Usually, but not always. Kholodilin’s meta-review finds 12 of 16 studies show supply declines. But Paris’s 2019 encadrement des loyers and New Jersey’s moderate controls showed no measurable supply loss. Design matters enormously: vacancy decontrol, new-construction exemptions, and enforcement intensity all shape outcomes.

The bottom line

Rent regulations protect current tenants but typically shrink supply and raise rents for everyone else.

The literature is not as one-sided as either advocates or critics claim. Rent controls do what they’re designed to do — stabilize rents for incumbent tenants and reduce displacement. But in most settings, they also trigger landlord exit, supply contraction, market segmentation, and spillover rent increases that harm the tenants they don’t reach. Design matters: new-construction exemptions, vacancy decontrol, and moderate caps reduce (but rarely eliminate) the tradeoffs.

Do rent controls reduce supply?

In 12 of 16 studies that measure it, yes. Diamond et al. found a 15% supply reduction in San Francisco. St. Paul saw permits fall 48–80% after passing a 3% cap. Berlin’s rent freeze saw supply declines five times larger than rent declines. But Paris and New Jersey’s moderate controls showed no measurable supply loss.

Do they raise rents in the uncontrolled sector?

In 14 of 17 studies, yes. Early & Phelps found uncontrolled rents 13% higher in regulated metros. Berlin’s freeze pushed rents up in neighboring Potsdam. Scotland’s freeze saw between-tenancy rents surge 14.3%. Demand displaced from the controlled sector bids up the free market.

Are “second-generation” controls really different?

Somewhat. Catalonia’s cap reduced average rents 5% without eliminating supply. Germany’s Mietpreisbremse moderated controlled rents but still produced spillovers. Paris’s encadrement reduced rents 3.7–4.2% with no supply loss. Modern designs are less harmful but not cost-free.

What’s the alternative?

The companion evidence browser reviews 45 studies showing that building more housing lowers rents — without the supply-side tradeoffs. The strongest policy frameworks combine supply expansion with targeted subsidies and tenant protections, rather than relying on price controls alone.

This evidence browser compiles 20 peer-reviewed studies, institutional analyses, and policy evaluations on rent regulation from 11 countries. Studies were selected for methodological rigor, causal identification strategies, and relevance to policy debates about whether rent controls achieve their intended goals without harmful side effects. The evidence base spans five decades of policy experiments across North America, Europe, and Asia. Compiled March 2026. Part of the Housing Economics Evidence Series.
