Three claims, tested against the evidence
The advocate says

“Rent control protects tenants from displacement.”

The evidence says: Yes, for current tenants — at a cost. Diamond et al. (2019) found San Francisco rent control reduced tenant displacement by 20%. But landlords removed 15% of controlled units from the rental market, raising citywide rents 5.1%. The tenants who stay are protected; the tenants who need to find housing pay more.

The advocate says

“Modern rent regulations avoid the problems of old-style controls.”

The evidence says: They’re better, but not harmless. Germany’s Mietpreisbremse and Catalonia’s 2020 cap are “second-generation” controls. Both reduced controlled rents 4–5%. But both also triggered spillover rent increases in uncontrolled segments, and Catalonia saw new rental contracts fall 10%.

The critic says

“Rent control always destroys housing supply.”

The evidence says: Usually, but not always. Kholodilin’s meta-review finds 12 of 16 studies show supply declines. But Paris’s 2019 encadrement des loyers and New Jersey’s moderate controls showed no measurable supply loss. Design matters enormously: vacancy decontrol, new-construction exemptions, and enforcement intensity all shape outcomes.

The bottom line

Rent regulations protect current tenants but typically shrink supply, raise rents for everyone else, and create hidden costs that compound over decades.

Across 38 studies spanning six continents and a century of policy experiments, the pattern is consistent: rent controls stabilize rents for incumbent tenants and reduce displacement. But they also trigger landlord exit, supply contraction, housing quality decline, apartment misallocation, reduced labor mobility, and spillover rent increases that harm the tenants they don’t reach. The costs fall unevenly — immigrants, newcomers, and in some cases racial minorities bear the brunt. Design matters: new-construction exemptions, vacancy decontrol, and moderate caps reduce (but rarely eliminate) the tradeoffs.

Do rent controls reduce supply?

In 12 of 16 studies that measure it, yes. Diamond et al. found a 15% supply reduction in San Francisco. St. Paul saw permits fall 48–80%. Mumbai’s tenant share collapsed from 82% to 21% over four decades. But Paris and New Jersey’s moderate controls showed no measurable supply loss, and Kholodilin & Kohl’s 106-year, 16-country panel finds the effect is weaker than expected at national scale.

Do they raise rents in the uncontrolled sector?

In 14 of 17 studies, yes. Berlin’s freeze pushed rents up in neighboring Potsdam. Scotland’s freeze saw between-tenancy rents surge 14.3%. South Korea’s cap backfired, raising average rents 17.7% via front-loading. Demand displaced from the controlled sector bids up the free market.

What about quality, misallocation, and mobility?

Glaeser & Luttmer found 15–21% of NYC controlled apartments are misallocated. Danish rent control extends tenancies by 6+ years, freezing labor mobility. Moon & Stotsky and Gyourko & Linneman document quality deterioration in controlled buildings. In Stockholm, queue-based allocation disadvantages immigrants. These hidden costs compound over decades.

Who benefits, and who pays?

Current tenants benefit — but unevenly. Chen et al. found white NYC tenants get $150/month more in stabilization discounts than Black tenants. In Lisbon, losing rent control pushed low-income workers into worse jobs. Favilukis et al.’s general equilibrium model finds vouchers and zoning reform dominate rent control in welfare. The companion evidence browser reviews 45 studies showing supply expansion lowers rents without these tradeoffs.

This evidence browser compiles 38 peer-reviewed studies, institutional analyses, and policy evaluations on rent regulation from 18 countries. Studies were selected for methodological rigor, causal identification strategies, and relevance to policy debates about whether rent controls achieve their intended goals without harmful side effects.
