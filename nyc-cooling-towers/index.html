The Invisible Threat

New York City has more than 82,000 cooling towers perched on rooftops across all five boroughs. These units are essential infrastructure—but without strict maintenance, their warm, misted water becomes a breeding ground for Legionella pneumophila, the bacterium that causes Legionnaires’ disease.

What Are Cooling Towers?

Hidden in Plain Sight

Cooling towers are heat-rejection devices that sit on the rooftops of large buildings—hospitals, hotels, office towers, apartment complexes. They work by evaporating water to remove excess heat from air conditioning and industrial processes. Most were installed from the 1950s onward, as central air conditioning became standard in commercial construction. In a dense city like New York, they’re essential: without them, large buildings simply cannot be cooled.

Why It Matters

Legionnaires’ Disease

The warm, misted water inside poorly maintained cooling towers is an ideal breeding ground for Legionella bacteria. The tower’s fan sprays contaminated water droplets into the air, where people nearby can inhale them. The result is a severe form of pneumonia that kills roughly 1 in 10 who contract it. Older adults and people with weakened immune systems are most vulnerable.

NYC’s Response

After a devastating 2015 outbreak in the South Bronx killed 12 people and sickened over 120, the city passed Local Law 77—the nation’s strictest cooling-tower regulation. Every tower must now be registered, inspected, and tested for Legionella at least quarterly.

Key Events
July–August 2015
South Bronx outbreak: 138 cases, 16 hospitalizations at one building alone. 12 deaths citywide. Traced to cooling towers at a hotel and housing complex.
August 2015
Mayor de Blasio signs emergency executive order requiring all cooling towers to be inspected and disinfected within 14 days.
2016
Local Law 77 takes effect. NYC becomes first U.S. city to require registration, maintenance plans, and quarterly Legionella testing for all cooling towers.
