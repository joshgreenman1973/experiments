Overview

This family tree traces the ancestry of Nancy Mauro, born April 24, 1973, in Thunder Bay, Ontario. Her family is part of the large Calabrian diaspora community that emigrated from Grimaldi, a small town in Cosenza province, to northern Ontario in the mid-20th century. The Mauro name appears among Grimaldi’s founding families in a 1651 manuscript — a lineage stretching back nearly a millennium.

The Fortino Name in Cosenza

The Fortino surname is common in Cosenza province. Notably, Giovanni (John) Fortino (b. 1935, Cosenza — d. 2011, Hamilton) was a Calabrian immigrant who founded the Fortinos supermarket chain in Hamilton in 1961 — the same year the Mauro family purchased Bennett’s Bakery in Thunder Bay. Whether the Fortino families are related is unknown, but both illustrate the deep Calabrian-to-Ontario migration pattern.

Deep Roots — Grimaldi Ancestors

Tracing the Mauro, Milinazzo, and Ciddio names as far back as available records allow.

The Mauri: A Founding Family of Grimaldi (~1027–1034 AD)

A 1651 manuscript by notary Giovanni Iacoe records that the Mauri (Mauro) family was among the founding families of Grimaldi, originating from the Santo Nicolò settlement when scattered hamlets consolidated into a single town after the Norman invasion:

“trassero origine le famiglie delli Maliti, dei Filippi, dei Rogliani, dei Mauri ed altri”

Grimaldi itself was founded between 1027 and 1034 AD. While this does not prove direct descent for Nancy’s branch, it establishes the Mauro name as indigenous to Grimaldi for nearly a thousand years.

The Milinazzo Name (~1444)

The same Iacoe manuscript records Palermo Milinazzo as having killed a count’s servant during armed conflict around 1444. This is the earliest known mention of the Milinazzo surname in Grimaldi — the family of Nancy’s grandmother Cristina.

Rita Filice’s mother, Chiarina Fortino, was born June 24, 1907 in Donnici, a hill town ~15 km southeast of Grimaldi in Cosenza province. She married Giovanni Filice and died May 25, 1959 (age 51). Giovanni survived her, dying in Hamilton, Ontario in the 1970s or early 1980s.

The Filice name is one of the most common in Cosenza province — a documented genealogy traces Fortunato Filice (b. 1850, Grimaldi/Maione) with descendants across Ontario, BC, Alberta, New York, and Washington. Grimaldi → Thunder Bay

The story of how a tiny Calabrian hill town became the source of the largest Grimaldiesi diaspora community in the world.

Grimaldi, Calabria

Grimaldi is a small town (~1,800 people today) in the Crati Valley, province of Cosenza, Calabria, southern Italy. Founded between 1027 and 1034 AD after the Norman invasion, when scattered hamlets consolidated into a single settlement on the slopes of the Santa Lucerna mountains.

Like most towns in southern Italy, Grimaldi contributed heavily to the Italian diaspora of the late 19th and early-to-mid 20th centuries. Poverty, limited farmland, and lack of opportunity drove thousands to emigrate.

Thunder Bay: Home of the Grimaldiesi

Thunder Bay is home to the largest immigrant community of Grimaldiesi anywhere in the world. Italian immigration to Port Arthur/Fort William (now Thunder Bay) began in the late 1800s. One of the earliest documented arrivals from Grimaldi was Vincenzo Veltri (b. March 19, 1861, Grimaldi — d. Jan. 31, 1913, Port Arthur).

By mid-century, the community centered around St. Anthony’s R.C. Church and the Italian Cultural Centre. As Raffaele Mauro’s life story notes, Port Arthur “was full of compari e paisani from Grimaldi.”

Bennett’s Bakery & the Persian Pastry

In ~1961, Raffaele, Vincenzo, and cousin Mario Nucci pooled their savings to purchase Bennett’s Bakery in Port Arthur. With it they inherited the recipes of original owner Art Bennett — including the Persian, a cinnamon bun with distinctive pink icing that became Thunder Bay’s signature pastry.

The three men ran the bakery for 17 years, and the Persian became a civic icon. Nancy’s 2022 novel The Sugar Thief is a fictionalized exploration of this heritage — “a secret pastry recipe that upends the lives of an entire immigrant family.”
