Three objections, tested against the evidence
The skeptic says

“When you build new housing, new people just move in and prices keep going up.”

The evidence says: No. Pennington (2021) finds rents fall 2% within 100m of new San Francisco buildings and displacement risk drops 17%. Asquith et al. (2023) show the supply effect “overwhelms” any demand drawn by new construction. New buildings absorb high-income movers who would otherwise bid up the existing stock.

The skeptic says

“Market-rate housing only helps rich people. You need to build affordable.”

The evidence says: Both help, but market-rate building has broader effects. In Austin, rent declines were steepest in the oldest, cheapest buildings (−11.4%) after a boom in market-rate construction. Mast (2023) traces the mechanism: 70 units in below-average-income areas open up for every 100 new market-rate units built.

The skeptic says

“Maybe supply matters in theory, but you can’t actually build enough to move the needle.”

The evidence says: You can, where it’s been tried. Austin grew its housing stock 30% (2015–2024) and median rents fell 17%. Tokyo builds ~150,000 units per year and has kept rents roughly flat for two decades while comparable global cities doubled. Stockholm, which restricts building, has 20-year waitlists.

The bottom line

The research is as close to unanimous as social science gets: building more housing lowers rents.

The effect operates through multiple channels — direct competition for tenants, filtering chains that free up older units, and reduced bidding pressure across the market. It shows up in causal studies from San Francisco to Helsinki, in natural experiments in Germany, and at citywide scale in Austin and Tokyo. The “induced demand” hypothesis — that new housing simply attracts more people, canceling out affordability gains — is contradicted by the strongest available evidence.

How long does it take?

Neighborhood rents respond within 1–2 years of new buildings completing. Moving-chain effects reach low-income areas within 5 years. Sustained, large-scale building (like Austin’s 30% supply increase) produces measurable metro-wide rent declines within 2–3 years.

Does new housing just bring in richer people and push prices up?

No. The three studies that directly test this — Pennington (San Francisco), Asquith et al. (11 U.S. cities), and Bratu et al. (Helsinki) — all find the supply effect dominates. New buildings absorb high-income households who would otherwise bid up the existing stock. Nearby rents fall, not rise.

Does it matter what kind of housing is built?

Even market-rate (“luxury”) construction lowers rents in older, cheaper buildings. The Pew analysis found rent declines were steepest in Class C (older, affordable) buildings — an 11.4% drop in Austin. Mast’s moving-chain study shows the mechanism: new buildings pull higher-income tenants out of older stock.

Is there any real-world proof at scale?

Two powerful examples: Austin added 120,000 units (30% growth) from 2015–2024 and saw median rents fall 17%. Tokyo, which builds ~150,000 units per year under permissive zoning, has kept rents roughly flat for two decades while rents in comparable global cities doubled.
