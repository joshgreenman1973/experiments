About this data

Sources: NYC Dept. of Records (digitized birth certificates, 1855–1909; death certificates, 1862–1948); NYC DOHMH Annual Summary of Vital Statistics, Table PC1, 1898–2023 (annual totals and 5-year averages); NYC DOHMH vital statistics datasets on Open Data (births by race/ethnicity 2007–2023, deaths by cause 2007–2021).

Data quality note, pre-1966: The main chart uses 5-year averages from DOHMH Table PC1 for years prior to 1966, which is why the line appears stepped during those periods. Individual annual figures exist in older PDF summaries but are not yet digitized. Borough-level breakdowns from digitized certificates remain limited to 1855–1909 (births) and 1862–1948 (deaths).

What this doesn't show: Migration — both domestic and international — has been at least as important as natural increase in shaping New York's population. The city's population surged from under 1 million in 1860 to nearly 8 million by 1940, driven overwhelmingly by immigration.

Historical caveats: Early vital registration was incomplete, especially before New York State mandated universal registration in 1880. Figures before that date undercount actual births and deaths. The historical death certificate data also lacks cause of death — only name, age, county, and date were digitized.

Not tracked: Religion, hospital/facility of birth, and occupation are not present in the digitized certificate indices.
Data from NYC Historical Birth Certificates, Death Certificates, DOHMH Annual Summaries, and DOHMH Vital Statistics.
